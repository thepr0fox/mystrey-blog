The Terrifying History of Lobotomy

Author: Anya

The concept of lobotomy first emerged in the late 19th century when a Swiss physician named Gottlieb Burckhardt began experimenting with a surgical procedure that involved drilling holes into the skulls of patients suffering from severe mental illness. Burckhardt believed that by damaging certain parts of the brain, he could alleviate symptoms of psychosis and other mental disorders.



However, it wasn't until the early 20th century that the procedure was refined and given the name lobotomy. In 1935, Portuguese neurologist Egas Moniz developed a new technique that involved inserting a long, thin instrument into the brain through the eye socket and using it to sever the connections between the frontal lobes and the rest of the brain. Moniz claimed that this procedure, which he called a leucotomy, could cure a wide range of mental illnesses, including schizophrenia and severe depression.



The procedure quickly gained popularity, and by the 1940s, lobotomies were being performed around the world. In the United States, the procedure was championed by psychiatrist Walter Freeman, who traveled the country performing lobotomies on thousands of patients, many of whom were institutionalized and considered incurable. Freeman believed that lobotomy could cure everything from anxiety and depression to homosexuality and even juvenile delinquency.


The Dark Side of Lobotomy: Horror Stories and Controversies

Despite its widespread use, lobotomy was far from a miracle cure. In fact, many patients who underwent the procedure experienced serious side effects, including seizures, infections, and personality changes. Some became apathetic and unresponsive, while others became aggressive and violent. Many patients were left with severe cognitive impairment and were unable to care for themselves.



Moreover, the procedure was often performed on patients without their consent, including minors and those deemed "mentally incompetent." In some cases, lobotomy was used as a form of punishment or control for those deemed "difficult" or "unmanageable."

As a result, the use of lobotomy became increasingly controversial, and its reputation suffered as more horror stories emerged. Critics began to question the ethics of the procedure, and some even referred to it as a form of torture. In 1949, the National Institute of Mental Health declared lobotomy to be a "doubtful" procedure, and by the 1950s, its use had declined significantly.


The Legacy of Lobotomy: Lessons Learned

Despite its many shortcomings, lobotomy did have a lasting impact on the field of psychiatry. It paved the way for the development of modern psychotropic drugs, which have since become the primary form of treatment for many mental disorders. Moreover, the controversy surrounding lobotomy helped spur the development of modern medical ethics, which now require informed consent, respect for patient autonomy, and a focus on minimizing harm.

While the history of lobotomy is a dark and often disturbing one, it serves as a powerful reminder of the importance of scientific rigor, ethical considerations, and respect for human dignity in the field of medicine. By examining the failures and controversies of the past, we can continue to learn and grow, and work towards a better, more compassionate future for those suffering from mental illness.
